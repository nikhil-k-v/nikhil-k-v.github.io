BRAILLE DISPLAY

FIRST PROTOTYPE

A functioning prototype of the display!

The display refreshes at an average range of 100-200 wpm, and has a final build cost of around 10$.

To enable such fast, precise, and repeatable actuating, the two servos utilize the De Bruijn Sequence to mechanically allow the "quickest path” to the next character.

For each character refresh, the servos generally have to rotate only 10-30 degrees, at an average of 17.6!

THE PROBLEM

After being shown the current, expensive solutions to internet-braille interfaces by a friend, I haven’t been able to stop imagining better solutions. Through a two year journey of stumbling across various ideas in mechanisms, software, and use-cases, (<- here a bunch of links, in a separate block maybe with arrows/lines pointing/connecting to them) I have finally prototyped a refreshable braille display that is extremely cheap, fast, and dependable.

Current refreshable braille displays use arrays of individual cells of braille, each dot having its own (apparently failure-prone) actuator - displays with 40 characters often have 320 actuators, a nightmare in many ways, especially maintenance. One single actuator failing could produce gibberish and render the entire display unusable. On top of that, the cost is often in the 4 figures, and in decades past that 4 would easily become 5, even 6 - not including maintenance.

The solution involves only 2 actuators for the braille, and a couple more for other functionality. By moving a refreshing cell of braille under your finger at a speed controlled through the pot/joystick, you can simulate reading braille manually off of paper, where you can choose your current speed and easily go backwards and forwards in the text. This prototype is functional - it can take any text and display the braille at a refreshing rate of anywhere from 0 to 200 wpm, backwards and forwards.

DE BRUIJN

Each servo is attached to a circle sector with 16 braille dots on its circumference - or rather 16 SPACES for braille dots. Some are “off” (0), while some are “on” (1), and the overall sequence is 000011110110101 - the De Brujin Sequence for (2,4), something which you may be familiar with if you are interested in algorithms, computer science, or even biology. The two sequences work together to produce any 4x2 braille cell, and then other motors in the case move the entire subassembly past your finger (at a user controlled, variable speed) to simulate the feeling of running your finger across a page of cells manually.
